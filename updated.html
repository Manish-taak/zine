ART & CULTURE PERSPECTIVES MUSIC

In Conversation

Shivam Punjya, Ankur Tewari, Rajakumari, & Mohammad Muneem

4895 Words

18-minute read

When we think of India, for diasporic Indians, there,s a subconscious affinity to thinking about the overstimulation of all our senses. As a country that, according to The Wire, has the largest diasporic population in the world at over 18 million, we've been conditioned to the contrived narratives of the smells, tastes, sights… and sounds of India.

The honking! The chatter of crowds! Moooooooo. But that's the problem. As we embarked on our research for this piece, we realized that the sounds of India couldn't be more averse to what we've grown with and we have some serious unlearning, and learning, to do.

We thought it'd be interesting to ask creators of sound - musicians, singers, and performers - a simple question: What does India sound like to them? And their responses were nuanced, complex, & eye-opening, and frankly, provided a new perspective to a tired boilerplate stereotype rooted in romanticized and Oriental characterizations.

We spoke* to three unique renowned individuals, each contributing to the global music industry in distinct ways, but unified through a powerful, visceral passion for a homeland: India. They're shaping the sounds of India that we're trying to learn more about.

First, we have Ankur Tewari, a Brussels-born singer, songwriter, music composer, and music supervisor, who is known for his work on films such as Slumdog Millionaire, Gully Boy, Gehraiyaan, and more recently, The Archies. Ankur is the Creative Architect and producer of Coke Studios Bharat. Ankur is also the lead singer of the band The Ghalat Family, known for their “acoustic guitar-based poetic ballads with hummable melodies.”

We then move into conversation with Rajakumari, a multi-hyphenate, Indian-American rapper, singer, and songwriter. She is known for fusing classical Indian music with hip-hop, and more recently, pop. Rajakumari is a Grammy-nominated artist and trained Indian classical dancer. Rajakumari has collaborated with John Legend, Madhuri Dixit, A.R. Rahman, Sidhu Moosewala, and Guru Randhawa, among others, released solo work, and serves as a cultural bridge between the East and West.

Lastly, we end our conversation with Mohammad Muneem, a Kashmiri singer, songwriter, and poet, who performs in Kashmiri, Urdu, and Hindi. He is the founder of the band Alif. Mohammad blends Kashmiri traditional and folk styles with contemporary Western genres, addressing themes of identity, struggle, and spirituality. He has won awards at the Dada Saheb Phalke Film Festival and the Indian Independent Music Awards. Mohammad also teaches songwriting and Urdu poetry.
